Spreadsheets never disappoint

16 May 2018

Photo by Vlad Sargu on Unsplash

For an example of sensible advice, you'd be hard pushed to beat Warren Buffet. Having built his fortune through his company Berkshire Hathaway, he knows a thing or two about buying and selling assets, or mergers and acquisitions (“M&A”) if we are to be formal. His recent annual letter to his company’s shareholders provides measured and entertaining advice when considering M&A deals.

M&A is as sexy as accounting gets. The hype, the thrill of the deal and the perception that high fliers work in M&A, draws hundreds of accountants to this area. For the modern-day CFO, M&A experience is a must-have. It is a core finance competency.

What M&A should come down to is paying a sensible price for the inherent value in the business. Too often, buyers pay over the odds only to write off the investment a couple of years later. The recent spate of retailer failures is indicative of this. Just this week another retailer, Poundworld, was put up for sale after its owners TPG Capital ditched a rescue plan. That’s three years, to the month, since the private equity firm paid £150 million for the business.

There are similar examples of House of Fraser, New Look and Prezzo. House of Fraser was purchased for £450 million in 2014 only to announce a Company Voluntary Arrangement (“CVA”) earlier this month. New Look, bought by private equity firm Brait in 2015 for £780 million, has subsequently been written down to nothing. Prezzo, another acquisition by TPG in 2015, announced a CVA in February 2018. Did the acquirers forget to ask how much they were paying for the investment?

In Warren Buffet’s letter to shareholders, he talks about how price brought a halt to most of their 2017 acquisition activity. He describes it as a purchasing frenzy where price seemed irrelevant to the acquirers. Like the housing boom of late 2012, the thrill of the deal, the pressure to complete and availability of credit can overshadow the value of the transaction.

“Why the purchasing frenzy? In part, it’s because the CEO job self-selects for “can-do” types. If Wall Street analysts or board members urge that brand of CEO to consider possible acquisitions, it’s a bit like telling your ripening teenager to be sure to have a normal sex life. Once a CEO hungers for a deal, he or she will never lack for forecasts that justify the purchase. Subordinates will be cheering, envisioning enlarged domains and the compensation levels that typically increase with corporate size. Investment bankers, smelling huge fees, will be applauding as well. (Don’t ask the barber whether you need a haircut.) If the historical performance of the target falls short of validating its acquisition, large “synergies” will be forecast. Spreadsheets never disappoint.” – Warren Buffet

The lesson: You can always find forecasts and synergies to justify a purchase, but are you paying a sensible price?

The number of retailers collapsing and going into administration is a stark reminder that business is hard, economic conditions are tight, and you don’t want to pay too much. Although today’s CFO is required to have M&A as part of their finance toolkit, perhaps the real skill comes from challenging those spreadsheets, not getting caught up in the hype and knowing what a sensible purchase price is. Warren Buffet says it best; those spreadsheets never do disappoint.
