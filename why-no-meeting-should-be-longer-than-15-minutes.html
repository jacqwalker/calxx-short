Why no meeting should be longer than 15 minutes

21 February 2018

Photo by Szűcs László on Unsplash

“We think, mistakenly, that success is the result of the amount of time we put in at work, instead of the quality of time we put in.” – Arianna Huffington

We live in a world which values being busy over being effective. We work long hours and brag about how busy we are. We wear it as a badge of honour even though we are becoming increasingly disconnected and unfulfilled. We don’t want to wake up late in our career and wonder if the amount of time spent in the office was worth it.

One culprit of the busyness epidemic is meetings. Most meetings are a waste of time. They are a tool to prove that we are busy and therefore are productive yet nobody, on their deathbed, wishes they had spent more time in meetings.

By default, we schedule 30 or 60-minute meetings and then find items to fill the allocated time, without questioning if it is necessary. This is similar to Parkinson's Law. Named after Cyril Northcote Parkinson, it’s the maxim that "work expands to fill the time available for its completion”.

“If I give you 24 hours to complete a project, the time pressure forces you to focus on execution, and you have no choice but to do only the bare essentials. If I give you a week to complete the same task, it’s six days of making a mountain out of a molehill. If I give you two months, it becomes a mental monster. The end product of the shorter deadline is almost inevitably of equal or higher quality due to greater focus.”
- Tim Ferris, The 4-Hour Work Week

The less time we have available, the more we will achieve. If we apply this thinking and shorten each meeting to a maximum of 15 minutes, we’d limit the discussion to what’s vital. Meeting organisers would be forced to focus on important items rather than 'nice to have' or ‘urgent’ ones. Urgent items are those items which shout the loudest, demand our attention, only to become a non-issue a week later. When we prioritise the important over the urgent, we'll spend less time in meetings. Our meetings will be more efficient, productive and timely as no attendee will feel comfortable arriving unprepared or late to a short meeting.

We will also gain back what I have termed ‘dead time’ - the time left when a meeting finishes early. It's too much time for a comfort break and too little time to start a new task. This ‘dead time’ quickly adds up to 30, 60, 90 or 120 minutes per day which we can't get back. Having shorter meeting slots will reduce the risk of dead time. Instead, we can use this time to find new customers, generate ideas, or improve current processes.

In conclusion, with too much time available, small tasks become big tasks. It will take a brave person to implement a 15-minute meeting, but the one who does will see a significant improvement in the quality of their work and life.
